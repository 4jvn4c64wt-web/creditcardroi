## Working title
**The gap between what a card promises and what it pays**

---

## Copy

Premium credit cards sell you on their annual credits. The Amex Platinum lists $1,500+ in stated value. The Chase Sapphire Reserve isn't far behind. On paper, a $895 annual fee becomes trivial — you're up $600 before you've thought about points.

The math works. The problem is the word "stated."

Every credit in the table below comes with conditions: a merchant you have to use, a portal you have to book through, a deadline you have to remember every month. Some conditions are trivial. Others aren't. The Venture X's $300 travel credit is nearly frictionless — book a flight, it fires. The Amex Platinum's $200 Airline Incidental credit requires you to select one airline in advance, hope your fees code as incidentals rather than base fare, and manually verify your statement. Same stated value. Different experience.

The table is built to make that distinction visible. For each card and each credit, it shows you the frequency, the type, and the restrictions — and it lets you input your own realistic capture rate. A monthly credit that you'd only actually use half the time is worth half its stated value; the slider reflects that. The net fee column is the number that matters.

A few patterns that aren't obvious until you look at all the cards together:

**Monthly credits are the most demanding category.** They require a decision every single month. Miss one and the value is gone permanently — there's no catching up in December. That's why a $10/month credit and a $120/year credit carry very different burdens even though they're the same number.

**Single-brand credits are binary.** The $84 Dunkin' credit is worth exactly $84 or exactly $0, depending entirely on whether you go to Dunkin'. Same for Equinox, Lululemon, Peloton. The stated value isn't wrong; it's just irrelevant if the brand isn't part of your life.

**Portal restrictions add a hidden cost.** Booking through a bank's travel portal to capture a credit is fine until you realize you've given up hotel status benefits, direct booking flexibility, or points you'd have earned booking directly. That tradeoff doesn't show up in the stated credit value.

None of this means the credits aren't worth capturing — they often are, substantially. It means the useful question isn't "what does this card offer" but "what will I actually use." The table is a way to answer that honestly.
