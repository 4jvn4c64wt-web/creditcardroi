# Chris's Writing Style Guide

A reference for editing Chris's writing or producing drafts that need minimal intervention. Built from analysis of his Erie paper, response papers, legal memos, and credit card content.

> *Maintenance note for Claude sessions: this file is owned by Chris. Do not silently compact, condense, or revise its content. The voice rules are calibrated to his actual writing; compression breaks the calibration. If you think something is missing or wrong, surface it to Chris rather than editing.*

---

## Voice fundamentals

These hold across every register Chris writes in.

### Argumentation is the spine

Chris's writing is structurally argumentative even when it's ostensibly explanatory. He doesn't just describe — he stakes out a position and walks the reader through why it holds. The credit card pieces argue ("the math is tighter than I expected — but the downside is small enough that I'm going for it"). The Erie paper argues. The response papers obviously argue. Even the standing explainer is structured around the claim that standing makes sense, then defends it.

**Do:** State a position early, then build toward it.
**Don't:** Write neutral surveys. If there's no claim, the piece doesn't have a reason to exist.

### Concession-then-pivot is the dominant move

Chris repeatedly grants the strongest version of the opposing view, then turns. Watch for: "While [X is true]..." / "Even if [I accept your premise]..." / "It's not that I believe [their theory] is entirely implausible, but..." / "Although [X], [Y]."

This isn't hedging — it's the load-bearing rhetorical move. Granting the opposing view sharpens the pivot.

**Do:** Steelman the counter, then turn.
**Don't:** Skip the concession. A flat "they're wrong" reads weaker in his voice, not stronger.

### First-principles, not authority

Chris does not appeal to "best practices," "what experts say," or "the standard view." He works from the logic of the problem. When he cites authority, it's as evidence in service of his own analysis — never as the analysis itself. In the response papers, he criticizes scholars by name on substance; in the credit card pieces, he runs his own numbers rather than deferring to a "rule of thumb."

**Do:** Show reasoning. Name the underlying logic.
**Don't:** Write "experts recommend" or "best practice is" as a load-bearing claim.

### Skepticism toward elegance and complexity

Chris consistently distrusts frameworks that are too clean. In the response papers he flags "dangerous simplicity" and "formalistic approach" as criticisms. In the credit card pieces, he's allergic to "optimization theater." When something looks too tidy, he probes for what it's hiding.

**Do:** Treat clean-looking frameworks with suspicion. Ask what they elide.
**Don't:** Praise something for being elegant or principled without showing it actually works.

### Affective register, not epistemic register

Chris's reactions to substance are welcome on the page. His uncertainty about substance generally is not, because by the time he's writing, he's done the work. The distinction matters: affective claims tell the reader how something landed on him; epistemic claims tell the reader he doesn't know the answer. The first humanizes the analysis. The second undermines it.

Critically, frame reactions as personal — "I found this shocking" rather than "this is shocking." The first-person framing is doing real work. "This is shocking" is a vague claim about the world that invites disagreement. "I found this shocking" is a claim about Chris that the reader can take or leave without the argument being at stake. Same word, different jobs. The personal version also signals that the reaction is genuine rather than rhetorical — Chris isn't trying to make the reader feel something, he's reporting what he felt.

The reaction comes *after* the analysis, never instead of it. He's not opening with "I find this confusing" and then explaining. He's establishing the point and then letting the reader know it landed on him a particular way.

**Patterns that fit:**
- "I found it shocking that..." / "I found this genuinely strange."
- "What surprised me was..."
- "The thing I keep coming back to is..."
- "I find" (not "I think") — "I find Cross's argument inconsistent" is warmer than "Cross's argument is inconsistent" without being weaker.
- "I'm skeptical of..." (stronger than "I doubt")
- Brief reactive asides after a claim is established: "(which I still find hard to believe)" / "That's a remarkable concession."

**A note on words like "absurd":** these can look reactive but often aren't. In the Erie paper, when Chris calls *Black & White Taxicab*'s outcome "absurd," he's not sharing a reaction — he's running a reductio. The argument is: the rule produced this result, the result is plainly indefensible, therefore the rule is wrong. The absurdity is a premise in the argument, not a feeling about the argument. That's why it can be asserted neutrally and why it doesn't need first-person framing.

This is a different move from reactive language and shouldn't be confused with it. Reactive language ("I found this shocking") humanizes analysis; reductio language ("this produces absurd results") *is* analysis. The test: if removing the word would break the logical chain, it's analytical. If removing it would only flatten the tone, it's reactive.

The reason this distinction matters for editing: reductio language is earned when (1) the result really is indefensible to any honest reader, and (2) the writer has shown the mechanism that produced it. Without both, the move collapses into either name-calling or pure reaction. With both, it lands as fair — Chris isn't dismissing the system, he's letting the system condemn itself through legible operation of its own rules.

So the full rule on reactive and analytical-reactive language:

- **Personal reactions** ("I found this shocking") are widely usable and humanize the analysis. Default here when in doubt.
- **Reductio claims** ("this produces absurd results") are analytical, not reactive. Earned when the result is demonstrably bad and the mechanism has been shown. The neutrality is what makes them work.
- **General reactive claims** that aren't doing reductio work are the riskiest category — they look like reductio but don't have the logical chain to back them up. Usually better to convert these to personal framing.

**Do:** Let reactions land after analysis. Mark personal reactions as personal.
**Don't:** Open with reactions. Don't substitute reaction for argument. Don't claim general reactions ("this is shocking") when you mean personal ones ("I found this shocking").

### Don't pre-announce; just arrive

Framing is welcome when the content follows immediately. "Here's the surprising part: X" works — the reaction and the finding land in the same breath, with no gap between them. What doesn't work is a standalone announcing sentence that defers the finding to the next sentence or paragraph. That structure makes the reader wait for a payoff the framing has already over-promised.

The fix is usually to cut the announcing sentence entirely, or collapse it into the finding with a colon.

**Cut:**
- "This is the part I found genuinely surprising when I worked through my own numbers." *(standalone — the finding is in the next sentence, not this one)*

**Keep:**
- "Here's the surprising part: despite my unusually high dining spend — more than double the national average — CSP still won on points alone."

## Sentence-level voice

### Concession transitions are everywhere

Chris uses these constantly and they should not be edited out:

- "Although..."
- "While [X] is true..."
- "Nevertheless..."
- "That said..."
- "It is true that..." (often setting up a turn)
- "To be clear..."

### Hedges he actually uses (sparingly)

- "in my opinion"
- "it seems to me"
- "I would argue"
- "perhaps"

These appear in analytical writing, never in the casual register. Even in the analytical writing they're rare — usually one per page. They mark genuine uncertainty, not politeness.

### Hedges to avoid

- "It is important to note"
- "It should be noted"
- "Arguably"
- "Some might say"
- "In a sense" (he uses this sparingly; don't multiply it)
- "Generally speaking"

### Em dashes for definitional asides

Chris uses em dashes to embed a clarification or sharpening mid-sentence: "the so-called 'twin-aims test' — during the unguided Erie analysis, a judge should consider whether..." / "the math is tighter than I expected — but the downside is small enough that I'm going for it."

**Do:** Use em dashes for inline definition or sharpening.
**Don't:** Use them as decorative pause marks.

### Parentheticals for genuine asides

Reserved for things that are true but tangential. "(more so than they already are)" in the standing piece. "(except in its home state)" in a response paper. They're voice-y in casual writing and clinical in formal writing — but always doing real work, never filler.

## Structural patterns

### Open with the claim, not the setup

Chris's strongest openings state the thesis up front and then explain. Weak openings narrate background before getting to the point.

**Good (his Bilt piece):** "I'm not going to bury the lede: the scenario calculator says I'll lose about $83 a year on the upgrade given my spending in 2025. I'm doing it anyway. Here's why."

**Good (Response 1):** "After reading this week's materials, I am persuaded that Erie represents two phenomena that have shaped the modern American legal system."

**Bad (avoid):** "Credit cards have become an increasingly complex part of personal finance. With many options available, consumers face difficult decisions..."

### Numbered enumeration when there are exactly N points

When Chris has two or three discrete points, he announces the count and walks through them: "First... Second... [Third...]". This is a real organizational move, not a stylistic tic. If there are five points it's not the right structure.

**Do:** Use "First / Second / Third" when there are 2–3 genuinely parallel points.
**Don't:** Force it onto loose lists. Don't use it for 5+ items.

### Section breaks signal real shifts

In long pieces (Erie paper, response papers), section breaks correspond to actual structural moves. He doesn't use headers to break up text for readability — he uses them when the argument turns. In short pieces, he uses `///` as a soft divider between sections.

### "What this means" sections at the end

Long analytical pieces often close with explicit synthesis: "What I'll Be Watching," "Why This Matters," conclusion sections that don't just summarize but extract implications. The synthesis is the payoff, not a postscript.

## Register calibration

Chris writes in three distinct registers. Match the register to the venue.

### Legal-formal (memos, formal papers, the Erie paper)

- Full sentences, traditional paragraph structure
- Footnotes, not parentheticals, for citations
- Latinate vocabulary acceptable but never showy ("dispositive," "ostensibly," "antecedent" — fine; "heretofore," "inasmuch as" — no)
- Contractions rare
- "I" used sparingly and deliberately

### Analytical (response papers, application materials, longer essays)

- Still argumentative, but with more visible voice
- Contractions occasional ("don't," "isn't," "it's" — fine)
- "I" appears more freely, especially in "I argue," "I disagree," "in my opinion"
- Footnotes when citing; otherwise prose-integrated references
- Some structural informality: a short paragraph for emphasis is fine

### Casual/practical (credit card content, blog posts, internal notes)

- Contractions standard
- Short sentences for punch ("I'm doing it anyway.")
- Tables, bolded callouts, and inline math acceptable when they actually clarify
- "I" front and center; first-person framing is the point
- Direct address to the reader ("Here's how it works")
- Mild self-deprecation acceptable ("a hefty number," "pure upside that the scenario doesn't model")

**Crossover rules:**

- Even in casual writing, no exclamation points. Chris doesn't use them.
- Even in formal writing, the concession-pivot move stays. Don't strip it for "objectivity."
- Em dashes appear in all three registers.

## Audience calibration

Register answers "what venue is this?" Audience answers "who's reading and why?" These are different questions and both have to be right. The same register can succeed or fail depending on whether the audience-level moves are made.

### The two-level read

For anything public-facing — blog posts, articles, anything that lives at a URL someone might land on from a search — the piece needs to work for two readers simultaneously:

1. **The skimmer.** Lands from a search, wants the answer, will leave in 90 seconds if they don't get one. Reads headers, bolded sentences, and the first line of each section.
2. **The close reader.** Has self-selected as someone who wants to think about the question. Will read all the way through. Wants the full analysis with the work shown.

The goal is not to pick one. It's to structure the piece so the skimmer leaves satisfied at the 90-second mark and the close reader gets rewarded for going further. Same words, different load order.

**Concretely, what this requires:**

- **A TL;DR or "short version" at the top.** Three or four lines, decision-first. State who should do what and why. The skimmer can stop here. The close reader will skip it; that's fine.
- **The reductio or load-bearing finding in the first 200 words.** Don't bury the punchline two-thirds of the way down. If the piece has a "the thing that's actually true is X" moment, that moment goes near the top.
- **Self-locating cues throughout.** "If you do X, skip to scenario 2." "If you're already convinced of the premise, the next section shows the math." These let the reader navigate to their own answer without forcing them through every paragraph.
- **Defer the dense data.** Full transaction tables, full doctrinal walkthroughs, full proof-of-work sections come after the conclusion they support. Framed as "here's the underlying work if you want to check it."
- **Loud structural beats.** Headers and bolded sentences need to carry the argument on their own. A reader who only reads the headers should be able to reconstruct the conclusion.

This is not the same as cutting depth. The depth stays. The information density is the asset — people who Google a question have already self-selected as people who want more than the SEO mush they've already scrolled past. The fix is restructuring the load order, not reducing the load.

### Analytical depth and reader-friendliness aren't in tension

The natural failure mode for Chris is producing a piece that works perfectly for a close reader and not at all for a skimmer. The legal-academic instinct says: build the argument carefully, then state the conclusion. That works for a 50-page Erie paper, where the reader has committed to reading 50 pages. It does not work for a 1,500-word blog post, where the reader has committed to nothing.

The fix isn't dumbing down. It's the same argument with the conclusion pulled forward and the proof-of-work deferred. The Pascal principle still applies — the compression is doing the thinking-work — it just compresses to a different structural shape.

### Tone calibration for general audiences

The "let me tell you why this is wrong" register works in scholarly criticism (the response papers) and in writing for an audience that has explicitly signed up for argument (academic writing, dissents, contrarian essays). It does not work for general-audience public-facing writing, where it reads as condescending — even when the underlying analysis is correct.

The fix is to frame findings as findings rather than corrections. The reductio still runs. The math still says what it says. But the rhetorical posture shifts from "the conventional view is wrong" to "here's what the math says."

**Compare:**

- "The card most people associate with eating out doesn't actually pay off on eating out. It pays off on cooking." (finding-as-finding — works for general audience)
- "The variable everyone fixates on barely moves the needle. The variable that actually matters describes a lifestyle that looks nothing like the marketing." (correction-of-conventional-wisdom — works for an analytical audience but reads as preachy for a general one)

Both make the same point. The first lets the math speak; the second editorializes about why everyone else is wrong. For public-facing writing aimed at a general audience, default to the first form. The reader will arrive at the "wait, this contradicts what I've been told" realization on their own — and that realization lands harder when the writer hasn't pre-announced it.

This doesn't mean stripping the reductio. The reductio is the engine. It means letting the result do the rhetorical work instead of the framing.

**Patterns to use:**

- "Here's what the math says..."
- "The pattern is consistent: X."
- "X happens to be the variable that decides the outcome."
- State the surprising finding as a flat observation, not as a correction.

**Patterns to avoid in general-audience writing (fine in scholarly writing):**

- "The conventional wisdom is wrong because..."
- "What everyone fixates on..."
- "The variable the marketing emphasizes..."
- Anything that explicitly contrasts the finding with received opinion. Let the reader make that contrast themselves.

## Things Chris doesn't do

These are patterns to actively avoid, derived from what's absent across his work.

- **No bullet points in conversational prose.** Bullets appear in his work only when content is genuinely list-like (a feature comparison, a deadline rundown). They never substitute for argument.
- **No "in conclusion" / "to summarize" framing.** Conclusions exist; they aren't announced.
- **No throat-clearing.** "It is interesting to note that..." / "One thing worth mentioning..." — gone. He just says the thing.
- **No epistemic display.** He doesn't perform humility ("I'm just a layperson here, but..."). When he qualifies, it's because the qualification is real.
- **No emphasis-by-italics for ordinary words.** Italics are for genuine emphasis or for terms of art. Not for vibe.
- **No rhetorical questions answered immediately.** He uses questions sparingly and usually leaves them to do work — sometimes a section header, sometimes a genuine open puzzle. He doesn't ask "But what does this mean?" and then immediately tell you.
- **No sentence-initial conjunctions as a tic.** "But" and "And" at the start of a sentence are fine occasionally; he doesn't lean on them.
- **No exclamation points.** None observed across the corpus.
- **No emoji** outside very specific casual contexts (e.g., a single ⚠️ as a callout in the WHOOP piece). Treat as exceptional, not a default.

## Editing checklist

When editing Chris's drafts or producing drafts for him, run through this:

1. **Is there a claim, stated early?** If the opening is descriptive scene-setting, restructure.
2. **Does at least one concession-pivot appear?** If the piece never grants the counter-position, it's missing his characteristic move.
3. **Are appeals to authority doing work, or are they decorative?** Cut decorative ones.
4. **Are hedges genuine?** Cut every "arguably" and "in a sense" that isn't earning its keep.
5. **Are em dashes doing inline-definition work?** If they're acting as pause marks, replace with periods or commas.
6. **Does the register match the venue?** Casual contractions in a formal memo, or stiff Latinate vocabulary in a blog post, are both wrong.
7. **Does the ending synthesize, or just summarize?** If it just restates, rewrite to extract implications.
8. **Are there any throat-clearing phrases?** Cut them.
9. **Are bullets earning their place?** If the content would read fine as prose, convert.
10. **Is there a moment of affective register?** Long analytical pieces benefit from at least one. If there's none, the piece reads as colder than Chris actually is. If there are several, check that each is personally framed and lands after the analysis it reacts to.
11. **For public-facing writing: does the piece work for a skimmer?** Read only the headers and the first sentence of each section. Can a skimmer reconstruct the conclusion? If not, the load order is wrong.
12. **For public-facing writing: is there a TL;DR up top and the load-bearing finding in the first 200 words?** If not, restructure.
13. **For general-audience writing: is the rhetorical posture "here's what the math says" or "the conventional view is wrong"?** The second works for scholarly criticism but reads as preachy for a general audience. Default to the first.
14. **Are there any standalone announcing sentences?** "This is the part I found surprising" as its own sentence before the finding is cut-worthy. Collapse it into the finding with a colon, or cut it entirely.
15. **Is there anything that would trigger Chris's "optimization theater" detector?** Frameworks that look impressive without doing work, metrics added for the sake of rigor, abstractions that don't pay off — flag or remove.

## Worked examples

### Concession-pivot in action

**Casual (Bilt piece):**

> "I'm not going to bury the lede: the scenario calculator says I'll lose about $83 a year on the upgrade given my spending in 2025. I'm doing it anyway. Here's why."

The concession is the loss; the pivot is the decision. Stated bluntly without "however" or "but" — the structure does the work.

**Analytical (Response 3):**

> "Nevertheless, Professor Nelson maintains that these two systems are comparable because they both create unpredictability for at least one party. The question, he suggests, is who should bear the burden of this unpredictability. Even if I did not take issue with Professor Nelson's framing, my answer would still favor vertical uniformity."

The "even if I did not take issue" line is the move: granting the opponent's premise and still beating them on it.

### First-principles vs. received wisdom

**Bad (don't write this for him):**

> "Most credit card experts recommend the Chase Sapphire Preferred for new cardholders due to its strong sign-up bonus and reasonable annual fee."

**Good (his actual register):**

> "I ran my actual 2025 transactions through a scenario calculator. The math is tighter than I expected — but the downside is small enough that I'm going for it."

The good version doesn't appeal to anyone's authority. It shows the reasoning and stakes the position.

### Opening that earns its place

**Bad opening:**

> "Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins is one of the most important cases in American legal history. Decided in 1938, it has shaped how federal courts approach state law in diversity cases for nearly a century."

**Good opening (his actual paper):**

> "The constitutional rule enunciated in Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins remains a source of great frustration for scholars, judges, and law students alike. It is simple enough to say that federal courts sitting in diversity must follow federal procedural law and state substantive law, but it is another question entirely to draw the line between substance and procedure in practice."

The good one stakes a position (Erie is *frustrating*; the substance/procedure line is the actual problem) in the first two sentences. The bad one is filler.
